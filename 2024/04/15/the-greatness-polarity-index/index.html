The Greatness Polarity Index

2024-04-15

It is often said that love and hate are two sides of the same coin. You can’t have one without the other. The yin to another’s yang.

Being a football fan can be the same way at times. We often say to ourselves, “our great [one side of the ball] is being wasted by a trash [other side of the ball].” To that, I posit that having a top-tier [one side of the ball] and an [other side of the ball] that you may find at the end of the driveway during spring cleaning season are two different forms of greatness.

Being average is easy. By its very definition, most things are average. But greatness in all its forms takes something extraordinary. To be at the top requires hard work and a level of genius. To be at the bottom requiresa cocktail of bad players, bad decisions, and an elite level of stubbornness.. They are two poles of greatness separated from the equator of mediocrity.

To that end I’ve created what I call the Greatness Polarity Index (GPI) and set out to find what teams have come closest to reaching the opposite poles of greatness while sharing a sideline. Who are our Robert Pearys, conquering the North Pole of superiority while looking across at their Roald Amundsen counterparts, trudging the trail of ineptitude?

The Greatness Polarity Index

The Greatness Polarity Index is based off of estimated points added, a metric that evaluates how a team performs relative to expectations. Both offenses and defenses are evaluated by this metric based on whether they score/allow more/fewer points than expected.

I only have data for this going back 20 years, so if you’re expecting a team from the 90s to be on here, I’m sorry. And of course football has changed a lot over the last 20 years. So we’ll be adjusting GPI to reflect the era in which a particular team played, and the formula will look something like this:

$$ GPI = abs[(TeamOffEPA - SeasonAvgOffEPA) + (TeamDefEPA - SeasonAvgDefEPA)] $$

We’re taking the absolute value of the sum of a teams differences from offensive and defensive EPA, so we should get a nice mix of good offenses with bad defenses and vice-versa. The offenses and defenses will be assigned a unit greatness polarity index (uGPI) for their component of overall GPI.

Without further delay, here are your top-10 teams in GPI over the last 20 years.

10: 2016 Texas Tech Red Raiders - 316.13 GPI

Kicking off our list are the Patrick Mahomes & Kliff Kingsbury led Red Raiders. This is your stereotypical Big 12 team of the 2010s and they led the country in plays per game, yards per game (463, which was 100 yards better than the second best team. There was as big of a difference between Tech and the second best team as there was between #2 and #34), and nearly every passing statistics, was top-5 in points per game (43.7), and Mahomes had more than 400 more passing yards on the season than the next closest passer (another entry on our list!), despite having fewer games played than anyone else in the top 5.

The problem was that the defense allowed similar numbers to what the offense put up. They ranked dead last in points (43.5) and yards allowed (554.3) per game and were tied for second worst in the country for opponent yards per play (7.1).

This squad finished 5-7 on the year, but gave us some memorable moments along the way.

In week two, Arizona State’s Kalen Ballage tied the NCAA record for most touchdowns in a game by one player with 8. Week eight gave us the legendary Mahomes vs Mayfield game that finished with a final score of 66-59 in favor of the Sooners after breaking all kinds of offensive records while both teams put up 854 yards of total offense with Mahomes passing for 734 yards. The low spot on the year came during week twelve when the Red Raiders lost 66-10 to an Iowa State team that finished 3-9 during Matt Campbell’s first season at the helm for the Cyclones.

Tech finished with an offensive GPI of u162.71, and a defensive GPI of u153.42 (second worst of any unit in the last 20 years) to give us our grand total of 316.13.

9: 2022 Iowa Hawkeyes - 328.15 GPI

Next up on the list is the first appearance from the Iowa Hawkeyes. Unlike the offensive fireworks we saw from Texas Tech to start the list, the Hawkeyes were anchored by a stout defense that was second in the nation in points (13.3), yards allowed per game (270.8) and rushing yards per attempt (2.8), and led the nation in yards per play allowed(4.0). The Iowa defense was anchored by Butkus Award winner Jack Campbell and first-round draft pick Lukas Van Ness.

For as good as Iowa was defensively, the offense looked as though they were facing their defensive brethren week in and week out when they trotted out the 123rd ranked scoring offense at 17.7 points per game. Quarterback Spencer Petras completed just under 56% of his passes for 1725 yards and just 5 touchdowns for the season. There wasn’t much of a reprieve in the run game as the team averaged just 2.9 yards per carry and 94.8 yards per game. The team ultimately finished third from last in the country at just 4.2 yards per play.

The team opened the season with a beautiful 7-3 win over eventual FCS national champion South Dakota State, posting a box score that looks more like a baseball frame. The teams traded field goals in the first and second quarters, before Iowa got safeties in the third and fourth quarters to invent the “Iowa Touchdown”. The Hawkeyes and the Jackrabbits combined for 936 yards on 21 punts, and Iowa’s Tory Taylor placed SDSU inside their own 20 on 7 of his 10 punts.

The other baseball score game on the season came on October 8 against Illinois, where The Illini took down Iowa by a final score of 9-6.

Iowa finished the season 8-5, but just 5-4 in the Big Ten West, good enough for a three-way tie for second in the division.

The splits for the sides of the ball were -160 uGPI for the offense and -167 uGPI for the defense.

8: 2015 Boston College Eagles - 336.23 GPI

Coming in at number eight on the GPI list are the 2015 Boston College Eagles, another team led by a stout defense. The Eagles’ defense was fourth in the nation in scoring defense, allowing just 15.3 points per game, only 0.2 points per game behind Ohio State and eventual national champion Alabama. They also led the nation in rushing yards per attempt (2.4) and yards per play (4.1).

On the opposite side of the ball, the Eagles posted a bottom-10 offense in scoring (17.2), completion percentage (44.4), and yards per play (4.4). If you like smash-mouth football, you may have enjoyed bits of this offense where they ranked 74th in rushing yards per game, but they ranked 95th in the country in rushing yards per attempt at 3.9 yards per carry.

The Eagles started the season hot with a 3-1 record and wins for Maine, Howard, and Northern Illinois, but they would go on to post a winless record in ACC play to finish 3-9 on the year.

In honesty, this team could be a lot higher on our list if not for the week two game against Howard, where the Eagles won 76-0 in a game where the coaches agreed to shorten the second half quarters to 10 minutes instead of 15.

October 10th was probably the biggest missed opportunity on the year, when BC fell to Wake Forest 3-0, despite out gaining the Deacs by 128 yards and holding them to just 5 first downs on the afternoon. But Wake knocked through a field goal after an Eagles turnover for the lone score of the ball game.

The Eagles offense posted a -146.67 uGPI and the defense stifled opponents for a -189.26 uGPI, for our grand total of 336.23.

7: 2014 Oregon Ducks - 336.62 GPI

The seventh entry to our list brings us the most successful team to crack the top-10. Oregon was led by Heisman trophy winning quarterback Marcus Mariota before going on to be national runners-up, losing in the inaugural CFP national championship game to to Ohio State.

Unlike the other entries on our list, the Ducks were actually at least decent on both sides of the ball. The defense boasted the 31st ranked scoring defense in the country at 23.6 points allowed per game, in a unit that was anchored by Arik Armstead and DeForest Buckner on either end of the defensive line. In fact, the 2014 Oregon defense is the closest to average unit we have anywhere on the list at just 45.14 uGPI.

The Ducks crack into our top-10 because the offense was just that explosive. Offensive coordinator Scott Frost engineered the second-best offensive uGPI of the last 20 years at 291.67. Mariota led the team passing for 4,454 yards on 42 touchdowns to just 4 interceptions while coming in second in rushing on the team with 770 yards and 15 touchdowns on the ground, and a 26-yard receiving touchdown to boot. The team averaged 45.4 points per game, 547 yards of total offense and 7.3 yards per play, all top-4 in the country for the season.

Oregon hit a speed bump on October 2nd when they lost to Arizona 31-24, but they would exact revenge in the Pac-12 title game where they destroyed the Wildcats 51-13.

The only times the Ducks were held under 38 points on the season were the two games they lost. Otherwise, they scored at least 42 points in ten of twelve games en route to a final record of 13-2.

6: 2022 USC Trojans - 337.14 GPI

Our next entry to the list gives us another Pac-12 high powered offense led by a Heisman winning quarterback. This time the USC Trojans led by Caleb Williams.

In the Trojans’ first season under head man Lincoln Riley they improved their record from 4-8 the previous season to go 11-3, including appearances in the Pac-12 championship game and the Cotton Bowl.

The offense was top-3 in points per game (41.4), passing yards per game (335.4), yards per game (506.6), and yards per play (7.2). This unit improved a lot from the previous year, thanks in large part to the transfer portal that brought them Caleb Williams, Mario Williams, and Jordan Addison, adding to a passing game that already featured Tajh Washington.

Defensively, however, the Trojans struggled to stop opponents, ranking 94th in scoring defense (29.2) and 119th in yards per play (6.5). This unit had good pieces, including Mekhi Blackmon and Tuli Tuipulotu, but Alex Grinch as a coordinator is a lot to overcome.

USC lost to conference rival Utah twice on the year, including a 47-24 loss in the conference championship game, before losing to Tulane in the Cotton Bowl in a 46-45 thriller. Outside of that, the Trojans won their other 11 games and post 40+ points in 10 of their 13 games.

The offense posted a uGPI of 236.94, while the defense had a uGPI of 100.2 for our total of 337.14.

5: 2023 LSU Tigers - 339.46 GPI

I hope you aren’t sick of Heisman winning quarterbacks being squandered by lackluster defenses, because we’ve got another team that fits that bill to start our top-5.

LSU’s offense was a juggernaut all season long, leading the nation in scoring (45.5), yards (543.5) and yards per play (8.4, a whopping 0.6 yards better than second place Oregon at 7.8). They were led by Heisman winner Jayden Daniels throwing the ball to an outstanding receiving corps that featured Malik Nabers, Brian Thomas, and Kyren Lacey. The uGPI for the offense came in at 237.86 which was good enough for the tenth best offense of the last 20 years.

Defensively, the Tigers were middling. I wouldn’t describe them as porous, but they weren’t quite average either. They ranked 82nd in scoring defense at 28 points allowed and 108th in yards per play, tying with teams like UConn, UTEP, and Georgia Southern. Their defensive uGPI came in at 101.6.

LSU handled business against lesser opponents, but struggled against higher tier foes going just 1-4 against ranked teams. They finished the season 10-3 including a ReliaQuest Bowl win over Wisconsin.

4: 2016 Louisiana Tech Bulldogs - 341.23 GPI

Staying in the bayou we have our lone G5 program coming in at number four. The Bulldogs were led by Skip Holtz in his fourth year as the leader of the program.

LaTech is another offensive power on our list where they were second in the nation in scoring (44.3), passing yards (363.4), and yards per play (7.6). The gunslinger for the bulldogs was senior Ryan Higgins who posted more than 4600 passing yards with 41 touchdowns on just 8 picks.

The problem came when the Bulldogs ran into the only teams to best them in the aforementioned offensive categories. Louisiana Tech participated in the only game between two members of this list when they faced off with Texas Tech on September 17. The Red Raiders got the best of that match up, taking down the Bulldogs in a 59-45 barn burner. This game saw 1,238 yards of total offense and 14 total touchdowns between the two teams.

The other team that beat them on a few offensive statistical categories was conference foe Western Kentucky who they faced in the conference championship game. The Hilltoppers won that game and C-USA on that day 58-44. That afternoon saw 1,163 yards of total offense and 12 total touchdowns between the two squads, including a pick-six from Louisiana Tech’s L’Jarius Snead.

Tech would finish the year 9-5 and 6-2 in conference with an offensive uGPI of 229.24 (15th best in the last 20 years) and a defensive uGPI of 112.0.

3: 2023 Iowa Hawkeyes - 344.91 GPI

Welcome back to Ferentz’s! You are our bronze medalists in the Olympics of polar greatness. You were not satisfied with 9th place in 2022, so you built both sides even greater the following year and for that I commend you.

Many Iowa fans (read: me) went into the season optimistic, thinking that the offense couldn’t possibly be more abysmal than in 2022. Somehow, though, Brian Ferentz accomplished that impossible feat, dragging the men down into the realm of which he is king: Dante’s eighth circle of Hell, the circle of fraudulence. Dante tells us in the Divine Comedy that Ulysses’ love for his father or for his son couldn’t overcome his desire for the vices of men. The story of Ulysses is that of the Ferentz’s as well. No matter the head coach father’s love for his offensive coordinator son, they could not together overcome their vice of punts. Many coaches on this list avoid punts in the way most people avoid cocaine. But the Ferentz’s are the Escobars of punts. Distributing the drug to the state, getting us hooked on it to the point Iowan’s adopt the mantra of “punting is winning.”

And like the white powder, the punts were fun. Aussie Tory Taylor’s punting yardage would have placed him 3rd on the passing yardage list for the season, en route to being a unanimous first-team all-American and Ray Guy award winner.

Iowa’s incredible defense paired with an equally but differently incredible offense set the Las Vegas record for the four lowest O/Us in history, with the season finally against Nebraska coming in at 24.5.

But who am I to question the all-time greats? Did Brian Ferentz reach his 325 point goal on the season? No. But the Hawkeyes went 10-4 on the season with a Big Ten championship game appearance.

My father once told me, “never argue with idiots. They’ll drag you down to their level and beat you with experience.” The Ferentz’s dragged nearly everyone down into their abode in the eighth circle of Hell and beat them on home turf and I can’t argue with that.

2: 2015 Texas Tech Red Raiders - 412.90 GPI

Iowa is not our only back-to-back teams to appear on our list. For the other honorees, we welcome back Patrick Mahomes, Kliff Kingsbury, and the Texas Tech Red Raiders of 2015.

Led by a sophomore Patrick Mahomes, Tech finished the season second in the nation in points per game (45.1), passing yards per game (388.2), and total offense per game (579.5). All these numbers only managed to get them the 36th ranked offense of the last 20 years with a uGPI of 181.27.

This team gets their big boost in GPI from their defense that ended the year with a uGPI of 231.63. The Raiders defense allowed the fourth most points per game (43.6), the second most yards per game (547.7), and the seventh most yards per play (6.9 (nice)).

The Raiders finished the season 7-6, which is respectable after facing 4 teams in the top-17 in the country.

1: 2018 Oklahoma Sooners - 441.08 GPI

The journalist Tom Junod once wrote that, “gluttony is harder than it looks. It’s listed as a sin, as something you give in to, when really its a skill, requiring not just hunger but resilience.” The 2018 Oklahoma Sooners are not a sinful football team, but a skilled and resilient offensive machine intent on racking up as many points as possible in a 60-minute football game.

This machine boasted talents like Kyler Murray, CeeDee Lamb, Hollywood Brown, Charleston Rambo, and Trey Sermon, anchored by Creed Humphrey in the center of the line. Their firepower led the nation in points per game (48.4), yards per game (570.7, 35 yards better than the second best team), and yards per play (8.7). In fact, the 2018 Oklahoma offense ranks as the best unit of the last 20 years, coming in with a uGPI of 293.20

Defensively the Sooners fell apart to allow more passing yards per game (294) than any other team in the country, in addition to being 17th worst in total yards allowed per game (453.8) and 101st in the country in scoring defense (33.3 points per game allowed). This netted them a uGPI of 147.88, good for the 27th lowest rated defense of the last 20 years.

The Sooners are one of our most successful teams on the list, however, winning the Big 12 and appearing in the College Football playoff semifinals.

But I don’t find that surprising given my original assertion that these are two different sides of greatness. Climbing Mount Everest and reaching the bottom of the Mariana Trench are both incredible feats of human achievement. When a team comes near to doing both we should not be shocked when they are great in other areas as well. I applaud and congratulate the 2018 Sooners team on finding our two poles of excellence.
